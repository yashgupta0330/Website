6 Must-Reads to Master UX in 2025

User Experience or UX is still the key to successful digital products in 2025. Whether it’s a high-gloss mobile app, responsive web app, or high-end enterprise app, users expect intuitive, effective, and enjoyable experiences. As technology continues to evolve and user expectations keep increasing, the work of UX professionals has never been more important—nor more exciting.

UX is not just design; it’s a strategy that involves psychology, usability, content, and interaction. It’s about understanding people, solving their problems, and crafting lasting experiences. And, as with any craft, mastering UX requires continuous learning.

This blog is your 2025 reading list—a personally selected list of six books that every UX beginner and veteran should read. From the basics to high-level methodologies, each book is chosen to make you smarter and enhance your skills in real-world projects. Whether you're starting your UX career or looking to stay ahead in the industry, these books will build your foundation and strengthen your expertise with confidence.

The Design of Everyday Things

by Donald A. Norman the Co-Founder of Nielsen Norman Group, a renowned American researcher and professor known for his expertise in Design, Cognitive Science, and as the director of Apple’s first UX group.

The Design of Everyday Things , introduces six fundamental design principles — Visibility, Feedback, Constraints, Mapping, Consistency, and Affordance. It’s a seminal text that redefines how users interact with everyday products by emphasizing usability, intuitive functionality, and a human-centered approach to design.

Key Highlights
  • Clarity in InterfacesHow to create interfaces that clearly guide users, making it obvious how to proceed.
  • Mental ModelsHow to align design with users’ mental models for seamless and predictable interaction.
  • Good vs Bad DesignHow to critically evaluate everyday objects and interfaces to improve usability.

The Elements of User Experience

by Jesse James Garrett User Experience Designer based in San Francisco, California and co-founder of the Adaptive Path strategy and design consulting firm. Garrett created a 5-plane of User Experience concept that breaks down the complexity of UX design into elements. 

The Elements of User Experience natural progression upon finishing The Design of Everyday Things. Garrett presents a framework that separates UX design into five interlinked planes and presents a systematic method for designing digital experiences. This book is a must-have for anyone who wishes to graduate from simple UX theory into using strategic and actionable design techniques.

Key Highlights
  • Strategy User needs and business objectives focused. It questions: Why are we creating thiit deliver?
  • Scope Specifies the functional and content needs—what the product does and what it contains.
  • Structure Encompasses information architecture and interaction design, determining how content is structured and accessed.

Don't Make Me Think

by Steve Krug highly respected usability consultant best known for his expertise in web usability. With decades of experience, Krug has worked with a range of companies—from small startups to large corporations—helping them improve the usability of their websites and applications. He is the founder of Advanced Common Sense, a user experience consultancy.

Don't Make Me Think, a Common Sense Approach to Web Usability is Steve Krug’s seminal book on user experience and web usability, first published in 2000 (with updated editions since). It's widely considered a foundational text in the field of web design and UX. The core idea of the book is captured in the title: a website (or app) should be intuitive and self-explanatory—users should not have to "think" about how to navigate it. Krug delivers this message with humor, simplicity, and clarity, making it an easy read for beginners and a valuable refresher for seasoned professionals.

Key Highlights
  • Usability Means Making Things Self-Evident User needs and business objectives focused. It questions: Why are we creating thiit deliver?
  • People Scan, They Don’t Read Web users typically scan pages for relevant content, rather than reading every word. Design should support quick scanning.
  • Conventions Are Useful Stick to common design patterns (e.g., shopping cart icons, navigation layouts) because users are already familiar with them.
  • Don’t Make Users Guess Every click should feel like a clear, logical step. Labels, buttons, and links must be obvious in their function.

Don't Make Me Think

by Jenifer Tidwell a software engineer and UX designer, along with Charles Brewer and Aynne Valencia, seasoned product designers with over 20 years of experience, co-authored one of the most visual and user-friendly books on interaction design.

Designing Interfacesdetailed guide to interaction design patterns—proven solutions to common usability issues. It helps designers build intuitive, attractive, and functional user interfaces. The book explains the reasoning behind effective UI structure and behavior. Whether you're designing for web, mobile, or desktop, it supports smart, user-focused design decisions. It's a book to be read by anyone interested in UX and who wants to cease guessing and create interfaces that honor user expectations and mental models.

Key Highlights
  • UI Design Patterns – How to create interfaces that clearly guide users, making it obvious how to proceed.
  • Feedback & Affordances – How to align design with users’ mental models for seamless and predictable interaction.
  • Consistency & Predictability – How to critically evaluate everyday objects and interfaces to improve usability.

The Content Strategy Toolkit

by Meghan Casey winner of awards for content strategy and a founding member of Brain Traffic. She assists organizations—ranging from Fortune 500s to nonprofits—in developing effective, scalable content strategies. With a UX and communications background, she bridges business objectives and user requirements through content.

Information Architecture and Content Strategy > is a guide for practical application of content strategy within UX design. Meghan Casey simplifies complex topics like information architecture and content planning for all team members. She covers content audits, voice and tone, content plans, and governance models. Casey emphasizes aligning content with user expectations and mental models. The book positions content as a core element of the user experience, not an afterthought.

Key Highlights
  • Content as UX Foundation Understand how well-designed content guides usability, accessibility, and user satisfaction. Discover how IA contributes to content findability and navigation on digital landscapes.
  • Creating a Content Strategy Roadmap Understand how to develop content from the ground up with methods such as content modeling, user journey mapping, and editorial calendars.
  • Voice and Tone Describe how to establish a cohesive brand voice that speaks to users across all platforms.

Lean UX

by Jeff Gothelf and Josh Seiden are product designers and UX specialists. They co-authored Lean UX, blending principles from Lean Startup methodology with user experience design. Their work empowers teams to build better products by making UX a core component of modern product development.

Lean UX hands-on guide for creating user-centered products in fast-paced, Agile environments. It moves away from heavy documentation and shifts toward rapid iteration, collaboration, and real-world impact. The book promotes early validation of ideas, user involvement, and measuring success by user outcomes. It’s an essential read for designers looking to work efficiently, adapt quickly, and integrate UX directly into Agile product development.

Key Highlights
  • Hypothesis-Driven Design – Learn to replace static requirements with testable hypotheses that reflect user behavior and feedback.
  • Cross-Functional Collaboration – See how Lean UX breaks down silos and promotes real-time teamwork between designers, developers, and product managers.
  • Design for Outcomes, Not Deliverables – See how Lean UX breaks down silos and promotes real-time teamwork between designers, developers, and product managers.
  • Iterative Testing and Continuous Learning – Embrace rapid feedback cycles to refine the user experience and stay aligned with evolving user needs.
